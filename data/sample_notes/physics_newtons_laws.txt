Chapter 3: Newton's Laws of Motion
Physics - Class 11

======================================
3.1 INTRODUCTION
======================================

Motion is one of the most common phenomena we observe in everyday life. 
From the movement of a ball to the orbit of planets, motion is governed by 
a set of fundamental laws discovered by Sir Isaac Newton in 1687.

Newton published three laws of motion in his landmark work "Philosophiæ 
Naturalis Principia Mathematica". These laws form the foundation of 
classical mechanics.

======================================
3.2 NEWTON'S FIRST LAW (Law of Inertia)
======================================

Statement: An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion 
stays in motion with the same speed and in the same direction, UNLESS 
acted upon by an unbalanced external force.

Key Concept - INERTIA:
Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion.
The greater the mass of an object, the greater its inertia.

Examples:
- A book lying on a table remains at rest until someone pushes it.
- A ball rolling on a frictionless surface would roll forever.
- Passengers lurch forward when a bus suddenly brakes (their body 
  tends to stay in motion while the bus stops).
- A tablecloth pulled quickly leaves the dishes behind.

Inertia of rest: Object remains at rest until a force acts on it.
Inertia of motion: Object in uniform motion continues unless a force changes it.
Inertia of direction: Object moving in a straight line continues unless a force deflects it.

======================================
3.3 NEWTON'S SECOND LAW (Law of Force and Acceleration)
======================================

Statement: The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the 
net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass.

Mathematical Form:
    F = ma

Where:
  F = Net force (measured in Newtons, N)
  m = Mass of the object (measured in kilograms, kg)
  a = Acceleration produced (measured in m/s²)

Important Points:
1. Force and acceleration are vector quantities (they have direction).
2. The net force is the vector sum of all forces acting on the object.
3. 1 Newton = the force needed to accelerate 1 kg mass by 1 m/s².
4. If net force is zero, acceleration is zero (object moves at constant velocity).

Momentum (p):
Momentum is defined as the product of mass and velocity.
    p = mv

Newton's Second Law can also be written as:
    F = dp/dt (rate of change of momentum)

This form is more general and applies even when mass is changing.

Example Problem:
A 5 kg object is pushed with a force of 20 N. What is its acceleration?
Solution: a = F/m = 20/5 = 4 m/s²

======================================
3.4 NEWTON'S THIRD LAW (Law of Action and Reaction)
======================================

Statement: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

When object A exerts a force on object B, object B simultaneously 
exerts an equal and opposite force back on object A.

Important Note: Action and reaction forces act on DIFFERENT objects, 
so they do NOT cancel each other out.

Examples:
- Walking: Your foot pushes backward on the ground (action); 
  the ground pushes your foot forward (reaction).
- Rocket propulsion: Gases are expelled downward (action); 
  rocket moves upward (reaction).
- Swimming: Swimmer pushes water backward (action); 
  water pushes swimmer forward (reaction).
- Gun recoil: Bullet is pushed forward (action); 
  gun kicks backward (reaction).

======================================
3.5 FRICTION
======================================

Friction is a force that opposes relative motion between surfaces in contact.

Types of Friction:
1. Static Friction (fs): Friction between surfaces at rest relative to each other.
   - Maximum static friction = μs × N
   - μs = coefficient of static friction
   - N = Normal force

2. Kinetic Friction (fk): Friction between surfaces in relative motion.
   - Kinetic friction = μk × N
   - μk = coefficient of kinetic friction
   - μk < μs always (kinetic friction is less than maximum static friction)

3. Rolling Friction: Friction when an object rolls over a surface.
   - Much smaller than sliding friction.

Laws of Friction:
- Friction is independent of the area of contact.
- Friction depends on the nature of surfaces in contact.
- Kinetic friction is independent of speed (for moderate speeds).

======================================
3.6 FREE BODY DIAGRAMS
======================================

A Free Body Diagram (FBD) is a diagram showing all forces acting on a single object.

Steps to draw FBD:
1. Isolate the object from its surroundings.
2. Draw the object as a simple dot or box.
3. Draw all forces acting ON the object as arrows.
4. Label each force with its magnitude and direction.

Common forces to include:
- Weight (W = mg) — always pointing downward
- Normal force (N) — perpendicular to the surface
- Friction force (f) — parallel to surface, opposing motion
- Applied force (F) — in the direction of push/pull
- Tension (T) — along the string/rope

======================================
3.7 EQUILIBRIUM
======================================

An object is in equilibrium when the net force acting on it is zero.

Types:
1. Static Equilibrium: Object is at rest and net force = 0
2. Dynamic Equilibrium: Object moves at constant velocity and net force = 0

Condition for equilibrium:
    ΣFx = 0  (sum of all forces in x-direction = 0)
    ΣFy = 0  (sum of all forces in y-direction = 0)

======================================
SUMMARY — KEY FORMULAS
======================================

| Formula         | Meaning                          |
|-----------------|----------------------------------|
| F = ma          | Newton's Second Law              |
| p = mv          | Linear Momentum                  |
| F = dp/dt       | Force as rate of change of p     |
| W = mg          | Weight of an object              |
| fs ≤ μs × N     | Static friction                  |
| fk = μk × N     | Kinetic friction                 |
| ΣF = 0          | Condition for equilibrium        |

======================================
IMPORTANT DEFINITIONS
======================================

Force: A push or pull that can change the state of motion of an object. (Unit: Newton)
Mass: Amount of matter in an object. Measure of inertia. (Unit: kg)
Weight: Gravitational force on an object. W = mg (Unit: Newton)
Momentum: Product of mass and velocity. p = mv (Unit: kg·m/s)
Inertia: Resistance to change in state of motion.
Friction: Force opposing relative motion between surfaces.
Normal Force: Force perpendicular to a surface, preventing objects from passing through it.
Equilibrium: State where net force on an object is zero.

======================================
EXAM TIPS
======================================

1. Always draw a Free Body Diagram before solving any force problem.
2. Choose a convenient coordinate system (x-y axes).
3. Remember: Action-reaction pairs act on DIFFERENT objects.
4. Static friction can take any value from 0 to μs×N.
5. Weight and Normal force are NOT always equal (only on flat surfaces with no vertical forces).
6. Newton's laws apply only in inertial (non-accelerating) reference frames.
